# Logical Fallacies

A fallacy is a reasoning pattern that feels persuasive but doesn't actually support the conclusion. Name the fallacy AND explain why the specific argument fails — naming alone isn't analysis, and a fallacious argument can still have a true conclusion.

## Reliable workflow

1. Reconstruct the argument charitably: conclusion, explicit premises, and necessary hidden premise. Use the strongest reasonable reading, not the easiest target.
2. Ask whether the premises are relevant, acceptable, and jointly sufficient for the conclusion. A weak link may be missing evidence rather than a named fallacy.
3. Quote or paraphrase the exact inferential gap, then name a fallacy only if its definition fits that gap.
4. Explain the damage: does the flaw make the support irrelevant, weaken confidence, or fully invalidate the inference?
5. State what evidence or revised premise would repair the argument.
6. Evaluate the conclusion separately. A failed argument leaves the conclusion unsupported; it does not prove the conclusion false.

**Output:** Present `conclusion → premises → inferential gap → fallacy label if warranted → effect on support → repair`.

## Fallacies of relevance (evidence doesn't bear on the claim)

- **Ad hominem** — attacking the person, not the claim. "He's a felon, so his math proof is wrong." Cue: character/motive substituted for content.
- **Appeal to authority (misused)** — citing a source outside its expertise or when experts disagree. A physicist on nutrition is not evidence.
- **Appeal to popularity (bandwagon)** — "Everyone believes it." Popularity tracks truth poorly.
- **Appeal to emotion / pity / fear** — feelings replace evidence.
- **Appeal to nature** — "natural therefore good." Arsenic is natural.
- **Appeal to tradition / novelty** — "we've always done it" / "it's the newest."
- **Genetic fallacy** — judging a claim by its origin rather than its merits.
- **Tu quoque** — "you do it too" deflects but doesn't refute.
- **Red herring** — an irrelevant tangent that changes the subject.

## Fallacies of structure

- **Straw man** — refuting a weaker version of the opponent's claim. Cue: restatement more extreme than the original.
- **False dilemma** — "either A or B" when C exists. Cue: exactly two options presented for a rich problem.
- **Begging the question (circular)** — conclusion smuggled into a premise. "The Bible is true because it says so."
- **Equivocation** — a word changes meaning mid-argument ("law" of nature vs legal "law").
- **Composition / division** — parts→whole or whole→parts leap. Each brick is light ≠ the wall is light.
- **Slippery slope** — asserting a chain of consequences without showing each link is likely.
- **Moving the goalposts** — demanding new evidence after each demand is met.
- **No true Scotsman** — redefining a category to dodge counterexamples.
- **Special pleading** — exempting one's own case from a standard without justification.

## Fallacies of evidence

- **Hasty generalization** — big conclusion from tiny/unrepresentative sample. Cue: "my uncle smoked and lived to 95."
- **Cherry picking** — citing only confirming cases.
- **Post hoc ergo propter hoc** — "B followed A, so A caused B." Sequence ≠ causation.
- **Correlation ≠ causation** — see the causal-reasoning skill for confounders and reverse causation.
- **Texas sharpshooter** — drawing the target around where the shots landed; finding patterns in noise after the fact.
- **Appeal to ignorance** — "not proven false, therefore true" (or vice versa). Absence of evidence is weak evidence at best.
- **Burden shifting** — demanding the skeptic disprove the claim. The asserter carries the burden.
- **Survivorship bias** — only successes are visible. "Every billionaire dropped out" ignores the dropouts who failed.
- **Base rate neglect** — ignoring how rare the thing is overall (see probabilistic-reasoning).

## Important cautions

- **Fallacy fallacy**: a bad argument doesn't make its conclusion false — it just fails to support it.
- Not every appeal to authority is fallacious: a consensus of relevant experts IS legitimate evidence.
- Not every slope claim is a fallacy: if each step is independently likely, the chain argument can be sound.

## Worked example

"Dr. Lee opposes the zoning plan, but she doesn't even live in this district, so we can ignore her traffic analysis."

1. Conclusion: the traffic analysis is wrong/ignorable.
2. Evidence offered: where Dr. Lee lives.
3. Residence doesn't affect whether the analysis' numbers are correct.
4. **Ad hominem (circumstantial)**. Correct response: evaluate the analysis on its data and methods; her address is irrelevant to its validity.
