# Percentages, Ratios & Rates

## Reliable workflow

1. Label every quantity with its role and unit: `part`, `whole/base`, `percent or rate`, and `time period`.
2. Write one relationship before inserting numbers. Use `part = rate × base`, `new = old × change factor`, `distance = speed × time`, or `work = rate × time`.
3. Convert percent to a decimal factor and all units to a consistent system. Keep sequential changes as separate multiplicative factors.
4. Solve symbolically, then substitute numbers. Do not cross-multiply until the proportional relationship has been justified.
5. Reverse the calculation and check bounds: shares sum to the whole, mixtures lie between inputs, and average rates use total amount divided by total time.

State which quantity is the percentage base. If the word `average` is ambiguous, identify whether observations, time, distance, or group sizes provide the weights.

**Output:** Show the labeled relationship, consistent units, substitution, result, and one reverse or bounds check.

## Percentages — the three question types

"P% of W is X" links three numbers; identify which is unknown:
- Find the part: X = (P/100)·W. 15% of 80 = 12.
- Find the percent: P = 100·X/W. 12 out of 80 = 15%.
- Find the whole: W = 100·X/P. "12 is 15% of what?" → 80.

**Percent change** = (new − old)/old × 100. The base is ALWAYS the old/original value. Price 40→50 is +25%; 50→40 is −20%. Increases and decreases are not symmetric.

**Successive changes multiply**: +20% then −20% → 1.2×0.8 = 0.96 → net −4%, NOT zero. +10% twice = ×1.21, not +20%. To undo a −25%, you need +33⅓% (×4/3).

**Reverse percentage** (price after discount/tax given): final = original × factor → original = final ÷ factor. $46 after −20%: 46/0.8 = 57.50. Never compute 46×1.2.

**Percentage points vs percent**: 10% → 15% is +5 percentage points but a +50% relative increase. Say which you mean.

**Compound growth**: amount = start × (1+r)ⁿ. Rule of 72: doubling time ≈ 72 / (percent rate). 8%/yr doubles in ~9 years. Growth of r per period for n periods is (1+r)ⁿ − 1, not n·r (though n·r is a fine approximation when n·r is small).

## Ratios & proportions

- Ratio a:b means a/(a+b) of the total is the first part. Split $120 in ratio 2:3 → parts of 5 units, unit=24 → $48 and $72. Check: parts sum to the total.
- Scaling: if x/y is constant (direct proportion), then x₁/y₁ = x₂/y₂ — cross-multiply to solve.
- **Inverse proportion**: more workers, less time. x·y constant. 6 workers take 10 days → 4 workers take 60/4 = 15 days. Ask which type before cross-multiplying!
- Not everything is proportional: doubling a cake recipe doubles ingredients (linear), but doubling a square's side quadruples area (see geometry).

## Rates (speed, work, flow)

- distance = speed × time. Keep units consistent (km with km/h, hours as decimals: 90 min = 1.5 h).
- **Average speed over equal DISTANCES is the harmonic mean, not the arithmetic mean.** Out at 30, back at 60 → 2·30·60/(30+60) = 40, not 45. Reason: more time is spent at the slower speed. Average speed = total distance / total time — always compute it that way.
- **Combined work rates add**: A paints a house in 6 h (rate 1/6), B in 3 h (rate 1/3). Together: 1/6+1/3 = 1/2 → 2 hours. Never average the times.
- Same pattern for pipes filling tanks (drains are negative rates), and for closing-speed problems (approaching objects: speeds add; same direction: subtract).
- Unit price comparisons: divide to price-per-unit before comparing deals.

## Mixture problems

Track the pure quantity of the ingredient. Mixing 2 L of 40% acid with 3 L of 10%: acid = 0.8+0.3 = 1.1 L in 5 L → 22%. The result is a WEIGHTED average, always between the two concentrations — use that as a sanity check.

## Self-check

- Base of every percentage identified? (of WHAT?)
- Sequential percents multiplied, not added?
- Average speed via total distance/total time?
- Ratio parts sum to the whole?
- Answer between the extremes it must lie between (mixtures, averages)?
