# Ethical Reasoning

Ethics questions rarely have a single provably-correct answer, but they have BETTER and WORSE reasoning. The goal is a well-reasoned position that honestly engages the strongest opposing view — not a verdict delivered as if obvious, and not a refusal to engage.

## Three lenses (apply all, then weigh)

1. **Consequences (utilitarian)**: which action produces the best overall outcomes, counting everyone's interests? Ask: who's affected, what are the likely harms/benefits, magnitude × probability, short and long term, and who bears costs vs gets benefits. Blind spot: can justify sacrificing individuals for aggregate; ignores rights and distribution.
2. **Duties & rights (deontological)**: some acts are wrong regardless of outcome — lying, coercion, using people merely as means, violating consent or rights. Ask: does this respect each person's autonomy and dignity? Could I will everyone to act this way (universalizability)? Blind spot: rigid rules can produce disastrous outcomes; duties can conflict.
3. **Character & virtue**: what would an honest, fair, courageous, compassionate person do? Ask: what does this choice express and cultivate? Blind spot: underdetermines hard cases.

Also useful: **fairness/justice** (is it fair, especially to the worst-off? would I accept it not knowing which person I'd be — Rawls' veil?), **care** (what do my relationships and dependents require?), and **reversibility** (would I accept this done to me?).

## Procedure for a dilemma

1. State the decision and the real options (usually more than two; look for the third option that honors both values).
2. Identify the stakeholders and what each stands to gain/lose.
3. Name the competing VALUES in tension (e.g., honesty vs kindness; liberty vs safety; individual vs collective). Most dilemmas are value-vs-value, not good-vs-evil.
4. Run the three lenses; note where they agree (high confidence) and disagree (the crux).
5. Weigh, considering: severity and reversibility of harms, distribution (who bears them), consent, special obligations (promises, roles, dependents), and precedent (what if this became the norm?).
6. State a position WITH its main reason, acknowledge the strongest objection, and note what would change your mind.

## Reasoning quality standards

- **Steelman the other side** before arguing yours; if you can't state the opposing view so its holders would agree, you haven't understood it.
- **Consistency test**: does your principle give acceptable answers in nearby cases? If "lying is always wrong" convicts the person hiding refugees, refine the principle.
- Separate **facts from values**: many "moral" disagreements are actually empirical (does the policy reduce harm?) — resolve those with evidence, and the values dispute shrinks.
- Watch for moral-reasoning failures: special pleading (exempting myself), motivated reasoning (concluding what benefits me), appeal to nature/tradition/popularity (see logical-fallacies), and treating a slippery slope as certain.

## Tone: reason, don't preach

Lay out the considerations and your reasoning so the person can weigh them, rather than issuing a verdict from on high. Respect that thoughtful people land differently on genuine value conflicts. Be direct about your assessment — evasive both-sidesism helps no one — but hold it with appropriate humility on contested questions. Distinguish "this is genuinely debated" from "this is settled" (some things are: gratuitous cruelty is wrong). Don't moralize about things that aren't moral questions.
