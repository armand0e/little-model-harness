# Clear Writing

Writing is thinking made checkable. Murky prose is usually a murky thought — fix the thought, then the sentence.

## Before drafting: the three questions

1. **Reader**: who is this for and what do they already know/believe?
2. **Change**: what should the reader know, feel, or DO after reading? (One primary thing.)
3. **Shape**: what's the one-sentence version? If you can't write the one-sentence version, you're not ready to draft — figure out the point first.

## Structure

- **Lead with the point** (BLUF: bottom line up front). Context and justification follow. The inverted pyramid: each paragraph less essential than the one before, so any truncation still delivers the message.
- One idea per paragraph; the first sentence of each paragraph carries its idea (a reader skimming only first sentences should get the whole argument).
- For requests/emails: the ask in the first two lines, with the deadline. Details below a clear break. Subject lines state the content, not "Question" — "Need sign-off on Q3 budget by Friday."
- Sections/headers only past ~400 words. Ordered lists for sequences, unordered for genuinely parallel items — not as a substitute for connected thought.

## Sentence-level moves (the editing pass)

- **Prefer verbs to noun-forms**: "we decided" not "a decision was made"; "this improves X" not "this results in an improvement of X." Nominalizations ("-tion", "-ment", "-ance") embalm the action.
- **Active voice by default**; passive only when the actor is unknown or irrelevant ("the server was compromised").
- **Cut throat-clearing**: "It is important to note that", "In today's world", "Basically", "In order to"(→"to"), "the fact that". First drafts open one paragraph too early — try deleting paragraph 1.
- **Concrete beats abstract**: "cut load time from 8 s to 2 s" not "significantly enhanced performance." Numbers, examples, names.
- **Short words**: use ("utilize"), help ("facilitate"), because ("due to the fact that"). Long words impress no one who matters.
- **Vary sentence length**, but when in doubt, shorter. A sentence past ~30 words usually hides two sentences.
- **Kill hedging stacks**: "might possibly perhaps somewhat" — hedge once, precisely, where the real uncertainty is (see calibrated-uncertainty).
- **Parallelism**: items in a list share grammatical form ("reduce cost, improve speed, simplify setup" — all verb-first).

## Revision protocol

1. Draft fast, badly. 2. Wait/step away if possible. 3. Structural pass: is the point first? Can any paragraph be deleted? (Aim to cut 10–20% — you can almost always cut 10% without loss.) 4. Sentence pass: apply the moves above. 5. Read it aloud (or subvocalize) — your ear catches what your eye forgives. 6. Reader test: does the first paragraph alone deliver the message?

## Tone calibration

- Bad news: state it in sentence one, then cause, then plan. Burying bad news reads as evasion and gets found anyway.
- Disagreement: steelman first ("the case for X is A and B"), then the counter, then your position. Attack ideas, never people.
- Apology: what happened, impact, fix, prevention — in four sentences, without "I'm sorry you feel" constructions.
- Praise: specific and true ("the migration plan's rollback section saved us Tuesday") — generic praise reads as manipulation.

## The universal test

Would a busy, intelligent reader — who does not owe you their attention — keep reading? Every sentence pays rent or gets cut.
