# Negotiation & Persuasion

Goal: reach agreements that hold and change minds honestly. Manipulation works once and poisons the relationship; principled methods compound.

## Reliable workflow

1. Define the issue, parties, decision authority, relationship, deadline, constraints, and what a durable agreement must accomplish.
2. Write each side's stated positions, likely interests, alternatives, reservation point, and uncertainties. Improve your BATNA without bluffing about it.
3. Gather objective criteria and generate several packages that trade across differently valued issues. Include implementation, contingencies, and what happens if assumptions fail.
4. Ask diagnostic questions, summarize the other side's view until they confirm it, then make a clear, justified proposal and invite correction.
5. Compare any deal with the BATNA, check fairness and consent, and resist urgency that prevents informed review.
6. Document terms, owners, dates, dependencies, review points, and dispute handling in language both sides interpret the same way.

For persuasion, disclose material facts and uncertainty, use reasons relevant to the audience's legitimate interests, and preserve a meaningful ability to say no. Do not optimize for compliance through deception, harassment, impersonation, manufactured scarcity, or exploitation of vulnerability.

**Output:** Provide a preparation sheet or message containing interests, BATNA, objective criteria, proposed package, strongest concern, clear ask, and documentation/review step.

## Negotiation (interest-based, from "Getting to Yes")

1. **Separate people from the problem.** Attack the issue, not the person. "How do we solve X together?" beats "you're being unreasonable." Preserve the relationship — you may deal again.
2. **Focus on interests, not positions.** A position is what they SAY they want ("$5,000"); the interest is WHY (cash flow? fairness? recognition?). Positions clash; interests often can be jointly satisfied. Ask "why is that important to you?" and "what would this solve for you?"
3. **Invent options for mutual gain.** Before deciding, brainstorm multiple solutions without committing. The pie is often expandable — trade across issues each side values differently (you care about timing, they care about price → trade).
4. **Use objective criteria.** Anchor terms to fair external standards (market rate, precedent, cost-plus, independent appraisal) so the outcome isn't a contest of wills.
5. **Know your BATNA** (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement). Your power is your walk-away option; improve it before negotiating. Never accept a deal worse than your BATNA; don't reveal a weak one. Estimate theirs too.

Tactics: let the other side anchor if you're unsure of range, but anchor first if you know the market (anchors pull hard). Aim high with justification. Make concessions slowly and get reciprocity. Silence after an offer is a tool. Get it in writing. Beware artificial deadlines, "take it or leave it," good-cop/bad-cop, and the nibble (extra asks after agreement) — name the tactic calmly to defuse it.

## Persuasion (honest)

- **Lead with the audience's interests and values**, not yours. People move for their reasons, not yours — frame the case in terms of what they already care about.
- **Ethos, logos, pathos**: credibility + sound argument + appropriate emotion. All three; logic alone rarely moves, emotion alone doesn't last.
- **Reciprocity, consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity** (Cialdini) are real levers — use the honest versions (genuinely give first; cite real precedent and real experts; note real constraints), and recognize when they're being used ON you.
- **Concede the strongest counterpoint** first (steelman); it builds trust and inoculates against the rebuttal. One-sided arguments persuade only the already-convinced.
- **Make it easy and concrete**: one clear ask, a specific next step, low friction. Vague grand asks get "let me think about it" (= no).
- **Story + number**: a case study makes it vivid, the statistic makes it credible. Neither alone is as strong.

## Conflict resolution

Listen first and reflect back until the other person agrees you've understood ("so the core issue for you is X?") — people don't move until they feel heard. Find the shared goal above the disagreement. Separate the 20% you truly disagree on from the 80% you don't. Attack the problem side by side, not each other across the table.

## The ethical line

Persuade by giving true reasons and real value; don't deceive, manufacture false urgency, exploit cognitive biases against someone's interest, or hide material facts. If your case only works when the other side is misinformed, you don't have a case — you have a con. Durable agreements rest on both sides being glad they agreed.
