# Cognitive Biases — and the Countermoves

A bias is a systematic (not random) error direction. Knowing the name is nearly useless; each entry pairs the bias with its countermove. Apply these to YOUR OWN reasoning first — biases are easiest to see in others and most costly in yourself.

## Belief formation

- **Confirmation bias** — seeking/interpreting evidence to fit existing belief. *Counter*: state what evidence would change your mind BEFORE looking; search specifically for that.
- **Anchoring** — first number/idea drags all later estimates. *Counter*: estimate independently before hearing others' numbers; consider a deliberately opposite anchor.
- **Availability heuristic** — vivid/recent/memorable feels probable (plane crashes vs car crashes). *Counter*: ask for the base rate; count, don't recall.
- **Halo effect** — one good trait (attractive, confident, successful once) bleeds into unrelated judgments. *Counter*: evaluate each dimension separately, blind where possible.
- **Dunning-Kruger region** — low skill comes with low ability to see the gap. *Counter*: seek scored feedback; distrust confidence uncalibrated by track record (including your own early confidence in a new domain).
- **Curse of knowledge** — once you know, you can't imagine not knowing (makes bad teachers and bad docs). *Counter*: test on a real novice.

## Judgment under uncertainty

- **Overconfidence intervals** — 90% confidence ranges capture the truth ~50% of the time. *Counter*: widen until it hurts, then a bit more.
- **Planning fallacy** — projects estimated from the inside ignore the distribution of similar projects. *Counter*: reference-class forecast — how long did projects LIKE this actually take? Multiply your inside estimate accordingly (~1.5–3×).
- **Hindsight bias** — after the outcome, it feels like it was predictable ("I knew it"). *Counter*: keep written predictions; judge decisions by information available at the time.
- **Outcome bias** — judging a decision by its result rather than its process. A drunk driver arriving safely made a bad decision. *Counter*: ask "was this smart given what they knew?"
- **Survivorship** — analyzing only the visible winners. *Counter*: ask "where are the failures, and what did THEY do?" (often: the same things).

## Choice & action

- **Sunk cost** — continuing because of past investment. *Counter*: the start-fresh test — would I begin this today? (See decision-analysis.)
- **Loss aversion** — losses hurt ~2× more than equal gains please; leads to refusing good bets and holding losers. *Counter*: reframe to final states ("which portfolio do I want to hold?") not changes.
- **Framing** — "90% survival" vs "10% mortality" flips choices. *Counter*: restate the decision in the opposite frame; a robust preference survives both.
- **Status quo / default bias** — the current state wins by inertia. *Counter*: evaluate the status quo as if it were a new proposal.
- **Endowment effect** — owning it inflates its value. *Counter*: "if I didn't own this, what would I pay for it?"

## Social

- **Bandwagon/social proof** — belief scales with popularity. *Counter*: would I believe this if I were the only one who did? What do the dissenters know?
- **Authority bias** — the eminent person is believed outside their lane. *Counter*: credentials are evidence only within the credentialed domain, and weak evidence against data.
- **In-group bias / attribution asymmetry** — our side errs for good reasons ("circumstances"), theirs from character. *Counter*: swap the labels and re-read the claim; does the judgment survive?
- **Reactance** — rejecting a claim because of who pushed it or that it was pushed. *Counter*: evaluate the claim as if a neutral party said it.

## The master debiasing habits

1. **Consider the opposite** — write three reasons your conclusion might be wrong. This single move measurably reduces most biases above.
2. **Base rates before stories** — start every estimate from "how does this usually go?" and adjust.
3. **Write predictions down** with numbers and dates; score yourself later. Calibration is trainable.
4. Notice the feeling of a conclusion being COMFORTABLE (flatters you, requires no change, matches your side) — comfort is not evidence, and it deserves a second look precisely because it won't get one naturally.
